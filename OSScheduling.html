OS Scheduling

Introduction

In this lab I explored different operating system scheduling algorithms and how processes are managed by the CPU. Scheduling algorithms are important because they control the order in which processes are executed and directly affect system performance, waiting time and turnaround time.

During this lab I worked with several scheduling algorithms including First Come First Serve (FCFS), Shortest Job First (SJF) and Round Robin scheduling. I also explored how Gantt charts can be used to visualise process execution.

First Come First Serve (FCFS)

FCFS scheduling is one of the simplest scheduling algorithms. Processes are executed in the exact order that they arrive in the ready queue.

In this exercise I calculated waiting times and turnaround times for multiple processes. I also created Gantt charts to represent the execution order of the processes.

One advantage of FCFS scheduling is that it is simple and easy to implement. However, it can lead to large waiting times when long processes arrive before short processes.

Shortest Job First (SJF)

SJF scheduling selects the process with the shortest burst time first. This algorithm can reduce average waiting time compared to FCFS scheduling.

During the lab I calculated waiting times, turnaround times and process completion order using SJF scheduling. I also compared the results with FCFS scheduling to observe the differences in performance.

SJF scheduling is more efficient than FCFS in many situations, but it can be difficult to predict process burst times accurately.

Round Robin Scheduling

Round Robin scheduling uses a time quantum to divide CPU execution time fairly between processes. Each process receives a fixed amount of CPU time before the next process is executed.

In this exercise I experimented with different quantum values and observed how process execution changed. Gantt charts were created to visualise context switching and CPU sharing between processes.

Round Robin scheduling is commonly used in time-sharing operating systems because it provides fair CPU access for all processes.

These calculations demonstrated how scheduling algorithms affect CPU efficiency and process performance.
